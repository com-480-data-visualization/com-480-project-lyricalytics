Understanding our data

Data Sources

For this project, we use three Kaggle datasets to analyze the relationship between musical success, song genre, and lyrical content:

  • Spotify Tracks Dataset — Contains detailed metadata on 89,741 Spotify songs, including features such as tempo, danceability, energy, and associated genres (e.g., pop, acoustic, rock).
  • 960K Spotify Songs With Lyrics Data — Provides lyrics for 955,320 Spotify songs, offering the textual data needed for sentiment and emotion analysis.
  • Top Hits Spotify from 2000-2019 — Lists 2,000 top-charting songs over two decades, helping us link lyrical emotions to commercial success.
Data Unification

When selecting datasets, we ensured all were based on Spotify data. The Spotify Tracks Dataset and 960K Spotify Songs With Lyrics Data were matched directly using track IDs. Top Hits Spotify from 2000-2019 did not include track IDs, so we matched songs by carefully cleaning and aligning artist names, song titles, and track features.

After merging, our final dataset consists of 80,274 songs with matching lyrics, including 1,868 top hits, and covering 114 unique genres. There are no missing or null values.

Emotion Analysis

For emotional analysis, we focused on songs with English lyrics. To achieve this, we leveraged Google's language-detection (using the langdetect library), and kept only songs identified as English. While most songs were fully in English, manual inspection revealed some multilingual songs with English hooks. We decided to keep them, after conducting another analysis, focusing on songs mixing Korean and English (done by a native Korean speaker), showed the lyrics and emotions be aligned between the two languages.

We classified emotions using the HuggingFace model SamLowe/roberta-base-go_emotions, which predicts 28 fine-grained emotion categories such as admiration, amusement, anger, joy, sadness, and surprise. We excluded the "neutral" category from our main analysis to focus on more emotionally expressive content.

To check the model's validity, we manually evaluated a sample of 100 songs and found an 81% human agreement rate, confirming the model's generally reliable performance for our purposes.
